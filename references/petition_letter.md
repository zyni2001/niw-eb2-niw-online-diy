# Petition Letter

## Core Structure

Use this structure unless the user asks for something narrower:

1. Opening and requested classification
2. Summary of request
3. Organization of evidence / claim-to-evidence mapping
4. EB-2 eligibility
5. Prong 1: substantial merit and national importance
6. Prong 2: well positioned to advance the endeavor
7. Prong 3: on balance, waiver benefits the United States
8. Conclusion

## Prong 1

### Best evidence hierarchy

1. Government policy / standards / official guidance
2. Official public case materials from recognized organizations
3. Outside reporting and news

### Writing pattern

Use this order:

1. State the risk category or national need.
2. Quote the government material directly.
3. Explain how the applicant's work maps to that risk category.
4. Optionally add a short corroborating sentence from official public materials or news.

### Example framing

- Prompt injection and adversarial manipulation are not merely theoretical risks; federal materials identify them as deployment and governance concerns.
- Trustworthy AI includes not only robustness and secure deployment, but also more verifiable evaluation when outputs must be checked rather than merely sampled.

### Keep news subordinate

News should support lines like:

- "These same concerns are already appearing in real deployment settings."
- "The outside reporting is corroborative, not the primary proof of national importance."

Do not make news the main Prong 1 anchor.

## Prong 2

### What USCIS wants here

- a coherent record
- evidence of real contribution, not just participation
- objective indicators of future capacity

### Best evidence hierarchy

1. Publications with clear author roles
2. Citation quality and follow-on uptake
3. Peer-review service
4. Awards and recognition
5. U.S. research continuity and realistic future pathway
6. Recommendation letters

### Strong subsection pattern

For each core paper:

1. title + venue + role
2. one sentence on the technical problem
3. one sentence on the main result
4. one sentence on why that result matters
5. optionally one short recommender quote

Avoid overly technical algorithm names unless they are essential to the result.

Better:

- "in a representative attack setting, performance dropped from 81% to 60%"

Worse:

- "under PGD with 20% perturbation on Cora"

Keep numbers when they clarify impact, but reduce jargon.

### Citation quality

Do not stop at Google Scholar totals. Show:

- who cited the work
- what research line they continued
- why that demonstrates influence beyond one-off citation activity

## Prong 3

### Main logic

The goal is not to say "I want to stay in the U.S."
The goal is to show:

- the endeavor is contribution-driven
- its value is not confined to one employer
- labor certification is a poor fit for this kind of work

### Strong themes

- innovation-driven research cannot be captured well by a fixed position-specific labor-market test
- the work benefits academia, U.S. industry, and deployment-facing environments
- continuity and flexibility matter in a rapidly evolving field

### Recommendation-letter support

Independent recommendation letters are often most useful here. Quote them directly if they say:

- the work is not tied to one employer
- a rigid employer-specific framework would not capture the broader value

## Style Rules

- Write as if the adjudicator is intelligent but not technical.
- Prefer "risk, result, and impact" over method jargon.
- Use direct quotes from evidence when they materially support the claim.
- Avoid self-congratulatory language; rely on third-party validation.
